## Overview
Accurate biomass resource mapping is a foundational requirement for sustainable energy planning, directly
influencing feedstock availability assessment, supply chain design, infrastructure siting, and long-term
renewable energy policy. Traditional biomass estimation approachesâ€”largely dependent on field surveys
and linear statistical modelsâ€”struggle to scale across large, heterogeneous landscapes and fail to capture
nonlinear relationships present in environmental systems.

This paper provides a **systematic and technically grounded review of machine learning approaches for
biomass resource mapping**, focusing on how data-driven models leverage **multi-source geospatial data**
to improve spatial accuracy, scalability, and decision relevance. The study synthesizes findings from
recent literature where **satellite imagery (optical, SAR, LiDAR), GIS-based spatial layers, and ground
inventory data** are integrated with modern ML techniques to estimate aboveground biomass across
agricultural, forest, and urban systems.

Beyond estimation accuracy, the paper emphasizes how ML-enabled biomass maps support **practical
energy planning objectives**, including logistics optimization, bioenergy plant site selection, seasonal
energy forecasting, and sustainability assessment. Particular attention is given to challenges such as
data scarcity, sensor limitations, spatial heterogeneity, and uncertaintyâ€”highlighting why ensemble,
deep learning, and hybrid ML frameworks increasingly outperform traditional methods in real-world
applications.

## Technical Scope and Contributions
This work analyzes and compares a wide range of machine learning paradigms used in biomass estimation,
with emphasis on their suitability for large-scale, policy-relevant energy planning:

- **Regression and kernel-based models** for baseline biomass prediction and interpretability
- **Tree-based ensemble methods** (Random Forest, Gradient Boosting, XGBoost) for handling nonlinear,
  high-dimensional geospatial data
- **Deep learning architectures** (CNNs, RNNs, LSTM/GRU) for spatial and temporal biomass modeling
- **Hybrid and ensemble frameworks** combining ML with GIS layers and biophysical constraints to
  improve robustness and generalization
- **Multi-sensor data fusion** integrating optical imagery, SAR, LiDAR, and field inventories to reduce
  saturation effects and prediction bias
- **Emerging directions** including transfer learning, uncertainty-aware modeling, and digital twin
  concepts for data-sparse regions

The paper further connects these techniques to downstream applications such as **biomass supply chain
optimization, renewable energy system design, grid integration, and long-term resource sustainability
assessment**, positioning machine learning as a critical enabler of scalable and evidence-based energy
planning.
